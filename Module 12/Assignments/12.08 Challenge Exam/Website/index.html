Simplify, Substitute, Solve

Recursion breaks down the large problem into smaller problems that can be solved with the same algorithm


A really good example that the courseware gave us was that of a teacher passing out papers. The teacher passes out papers to the students at the head of the columns, then those students in turn pass the papers out backwards until the job is complete.


Another example of recursion deals with trees. From the trunk branches grow outwards, and then from those branches more branches grow, and so on

Divide and Conquer

Divide and conquer is fundamental to recursion. By dividing up the problem into smaller problems, the recursive program is able to do its job.


This year I competed in Battlecode. The contestants wrote software robots that battled each other. One strategy that proved effective was dividing and conquering. The team would divide it's large group into smaller groups to take down the enemy. The large group and the small groups had the same goal, but they were dealing with individual problems



Divide and conquer is also similar to how the students in AP Computer science dealt with 12.07: Working Together. They split up into pairs to work on a single problem, but each person was working on a specialized task to accomplish the goal.


The divide and conquer technique is essential to computer programming and teamwork. At hackathons I have to collaborate with my team. We break down the goal of our project into pieces and then delegate. From there we break down our individual delegations into separate problems that we can solve as we go along
